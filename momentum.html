Cross-Sectional 12-1 Momentum in U.S. Large Caps

Shaotong (Tom) Li  ·   · 

Abstract

This paper tests whether a stock's past 12-month return, excluding the most recent month, predicts its cross-sectional return over the following month within a point-in-time S&P 500 universe. Ranking within each sector into quintiles, the top quintile earns a gross Sharpe of 1.05, yet a market-neutral long-short of the extremes retains only a modest, cost-sensitive premium, and the monthly rank information coefficient is positive but weak. Because the universe is survivorship-free (delisted names retained), these estimates are deliberately conservative; results are reported gross and net of a simple transaction-cost model, with limitations stated plainly.

Headline results

1. Data and signal construction

The universe is point-in-time: S&P 500 membership is reconstructed month by month from historical constituent lists, and delisted names are retained with prices back-filled from a separate vendor, so the backtest carries no survivorship bias. Prices are cleaned for OHLC consistency, non-positive values, and zero-volume flats; all signals and forward returns are shifted strictly within each ticker to preclude look-ahead.

3. Limitations

The univariate predictive regression's naïve t-statistic of 2.6 falls to 0.97 once standard errors are clustered by date, i.e. below conventional significance once cross-sectional correlation is acknowledged. Survivorship bias is already addressed by the point-in-time universe; three caveats remain:

  • Close-to-close returns. No open/VWAP execution modeling; realized fills would differ.
  • Constant linear costs. Ten basis points per side omits market impact, borrow, and spread dynamics.
  • Short sample, one regime. Roughly nine years spanning a single, technology-led bull market; the estimates need not generalize to other environments.
